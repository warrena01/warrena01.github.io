Agent Based Modelling

Schelling's model of segregation

An Agent Based Model (ABM) is a computer simulation that is used to replicate real life. In GIS applications, more often than not the “agents” are simulated people or animals, who’s behaviour is programmed into the model so that we can see what patterns are caused by those individual behaviours at the population level. As illustrated by Dr Johnny Huck, a key use of ABM can be in investigating the impact of segregation upon mobility. His research focuses on North Belfast, and uses GPS tracking data for c.200 people and questionnaire and Participatory GIS data from c.500 people. Analysis of this dataset is used to create a population of agents that then move around a map of North Belfast so that we can observe their behaviour at the population level, and then examine how their behaviour changes if we make some form of intervention (such as removing a peace wall). Using this model, we can test interventions (e.g. the removal of peace walls that divide communities) before trying them out in real life, which allows us to catch problems and optimise solutions ahead of time!

ABMs in geography are used to provide a bottom-up understanding of either urban or natural systems. This means that we use our understanding of how individuals behave in order to understand the implications of those behaviours at the population level. Broadly speaking, they are generally used to answer one of two types of question:

1. Understanding how a phenomenon occurs (i.e. how a pattern such as segregation develops)
2. Evaluating the effect of some form of change or intervention upon a population (i.e. how removing a peace wall might affect segregated communities)

This work uses Schelling's model of segregation to demonstrate this use of programming in GIS.

Heatmap Analysis of Crime in Leeds

An analysis of crime hotspots for antisocial behaviour, sexual assault and violence around Leeds city centre

Discussion

This density heatmap map shows hotspots for sexual assault, violence and antisocial behaviour around Leeds city centre in October 2019 using publically available data from West Yorkshire Police. The maps premise is to highlight inclusive spaces, and thus the aforementioned crime data was chosen as I believe these crimes most hinder a space being safe and thus inclusive, and a heatmap comparing these crimes allows me to fulfil this maps purpose. The map shows the city centre to be the least inclusive space with inclusivity improving towards the periphery

Whilst point data was used as an origin for my final map, it wasn’t used as the cartographic approach due to the data not being precise - creating a misleading effect. Choropleths remove this effect, but the reliance on LSAO (Lower Super Output Area) boundaries misrepresent the data. In addition, these boundaries are often politically contested, meaning the redrawing of boundaries could have changed my results at any time. This is due to the LSAO boundaries being arbitrarily created from census data sets (which don’t have a crime focus, hence removing suitability to this map), and as a result of this it gives the impression of instantaneous crime level change across LSAO boundaries and uniformity in crimes across an LSAO. These visual impressions are still consistent in thiessen polygons too, despite boundaries being constructed from the data. Therefore, heatmaps proved most suitable as they removed the imprecision of point data and without a reliance on inputted boundaries, the heatmap can show change both within, and between areas, without sharp changes across a boundary

In the production of my map I chose a ‘plasma’ colour scheme, within a singleband pseudo colour render, to best emphasise the changing density of crimes, whilst also lowering the opacity of the layer to allow the raster layer to provide locational context to the data. A smaller map and legend was used to allow for inner city Leeds and the wider Yorkshire area to be compared and contextualised. The map relied upon the raster calculator tool, which allowed me to compare two density surfaces (produced using Kernel density tool), to identify areas within which both crimes occurred. With recognition of maps as ‘truths’, I created the original heatmaps map with a radii parameter of 200m, to limit creating a map as a ‘proposition’; thus allowing the raster calculator to make a map as a ‘truth’ when combining the two layers (Crang, 2014; Crampton, 2014). Further, the transparency of the heatmap output for no crime was 100% to ensure the raster calculator output only highlighted appropriate locations. However, whilst I believe my inputs created an accurate representation of the data, it is possible I inadvertently created a misleading map.

This map has proven useful in highlighting inclusive and exclusive spaces and could aid further study into motivations or casual factors for exclusivity in these areas. However, whilst police data is the best source, with research suggesting 63% of sexual assault cases not being reported, the map is limited by the data (Engel, 2018)
